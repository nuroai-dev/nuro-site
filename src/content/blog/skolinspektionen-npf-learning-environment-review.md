## The short answer

Sweden's state school inspectorate, Skolinspektionen, does not only audit paperwork. It sends inspectors into classrooms. In a thematic quality review published on 23 November 2023, [Skolors arbete för en tillgänglig lärmiljö för elever med neuropsykiatriska svårigheter](https://www.skolinspektionen.se/beslut-rapporter/publikationer/kvalitetsgranskning/2023/skolors-arbete-for-en-tillganglig-larmiljo-for-elever-med-neuropsykiatriska-svarigheter/) (schools' work on an accessible learning environment for students with neuropsychiatric difficulties), it looked at 30 grundskolor in years 7 to 9, through interviews with principals, teachers and elevhälsa, lesson observations, interviews with students, and document studies.

The headline finding, in the inspectorate's own words: "Två av tre skolor utformar inte undervisningen efter elevernas behov." At two in three of the inspected schools, teaching was not designed around students' needs. And "var tredje skola identifierar inte hinder för elever i lärmiljön": one in three did not even identify the barriers students meet in the learning environment. This is not a survey of opinions or a funding complaint. It is the state's own inspector describing what it saw in the room.

## What the inspector actually found

Three findings stand out.

First, teaching that does not start from the student. When two in three schools do not design teaching around students' needs, the first tier of support, the [ledning och stimulans and extra anpassningar every student is owed inside ordinary teaching](/blog/what-do-extra-anpassningar-look-like), is not happening reliably. The inspectorate put the cause plainly: "Lärarna identifierar sällan vilka hinder eleverna riskerar att möta i undervisningen." Teachers rarely identify the barriers a student risks meeting. You cannot remove an obstacle you have not seen, and [whether teachers are equipped to see them is its own documented problem](/blog/are-teachers-prepared-for-neurodivergent-students).

Second, and most uncomfortable, attitudes. The inspectorate wrote that "på flera av skolorna framkom dessutom exempel på negativa inställningar gentemot elever med neuropsykiatriska svårigheter både från personal och från elever." At several schools, negative attitudes toward students with neuropsychiatric difficulties came from staff and from other students. It named why this matters: "att negativa föreställningar uttrycks är problematiskt då elever med neuropsykiatriska svårigheter ofta redan möter fysiska, pedagogiska och sociala hinder i skolan." Negative beliefs land on students who already face physical, pedagogical and social barriers. This is the social layer of an [accessible learning environment](/blog/what-is-an-accessible-learning-environment), and it is the one that is hardest to legislate and easiest to lose.

Third, a detail in the scope that matters more than it looks. The inspectorate deliberately defined "neuropsychiatric difficulties" to cover more than diagnosed students: "det innefattar både elever med bekräftad neuropsykiatrisk funktionsnedsättning, och elever utan diagnos som kan möta liknande utmaningar i skolmiljön." Both students with a confirmed diagnosis and students without one who meet similar challenges. That is the same principle Swedish law already sets: [support is owed on the basis of need, not a label](/blog/support-without-a-diagnosis-school). The inspector built its whole review around it.

## Why it reinforces the same gap

This review sits alongside the inspectorate's broader conclusion, in its [2025 annual report, that the duty to support students is not always met](/blog/swedens-school-inspectorate-2025-support-gap). The difference is altitude. The annual report is the system view. This granskning is the classroom view, and it points at the same place: the right exists, the delivery does not. A third of the reviewed schools did well, which is worth saying. But two in three did not build teaching around the students in front of them, and that is [the gap between a right on paper and a lesson in practice](/blog/the-right-exists-the-system-doesnt) that this blog keeps returning to.

The inspectorate's finding is not that teachers do not care. It is that seeing every student's barriers, and designing teaching around them, is genuinely hard to do for a full class, lesson after lesson, by hand. That is exactly the work Nuro is built to carry: helping a teacher see what each student needs and adapt the material to it, so an accessible learning environment is something a school can actually deliver, not just something an inspector keeps finding it did not.
