## The short answer

Bildstöd, visual support, and AKK, alternativ och kompletterande kommunikation, are ways of making communication and information visible rather than leaving them to spoken words alone. Sweden's specialist authority for education, SPSM, puts the idea in one line: "Alla kan inte tala, men alla kan kommunicera på något sätt", not everyone can speak, but everyone can communicate somehow. AKK, it says, "kan användas tillsammans med, eller helt ersätta talat språk", can be used alongside speech or replace it entirely ([SPSM](https://www.spsm.se/stod-och-rad/sprak-och-kommunikation/alternativ-och-kompletterande-kommunikation/)).

The two words overlap. AKK is the broad umbrella, signs, symbols, pictures, photos and communication boards for anyone who struggles to understand or use speech. Bildstöd is the visual slice of it: the pictures, schedules and symbols that make the day and the task visible. Most Swedish classrooms use bildstöd long before anyone calls it AKK.

## Why visual support helps neurodivergent students

The reflex is to think visual support is only for students who cannot talk. In practice it helps students who speak perfectly well, for three reasons:

- **Predictability.** A visual schedule answers "what are we doing now, for how long, and what comes after" without the student having to hold it in memory or keep asking. For an autistic student that removes the stress of not knowing; for a student with ADHD it does the time-keeping their working memory struggles to hold.
- **Lower language and memory load.** A picture or symbol holds an instruction externally, so the student does not have to decode a wall of text or remember a spoken sentence before they can start. That is direct relief for dyslexia and for anyone whose attention slips.
- **A channel when speech fails.** A student who is overwhelmed, or who does not use speech, can point or choose a symbol instead of being stuck. Research shows visual supports such as schedules and social stories make it easier for autistic people to take in information.

## The common forms

- **Bildschema**, a visual schedule: the day or the lesson laid out as a sequence of pictures.
- **Kommunikationskarta**, a communication board: symbols the student points to in order to be understood.
- **Ritprat**, drawn conversations, and **sociala berättelser**, social stories: drawing a situation out to make sense of it together.
- **Pictogram**, SPSM's free symbol system, available to download at pictogram.se and used in schedules and communication boards across Swedish schools.
- **Sequence pictures and visual timers**, which turn "soon" and "a little while" into something a student can actually see.

## The myth about speech

The most common worry is that giving a child pictures or signs will hold their talking back. The evidence points the other way. There is no research suggesting AKK inhibits speech development, and it often supports it, because it takes the pressure off and gives the child a working way to communicate while spoken language develops ([Autismforum](https://www.autismforum.se/stod-och-insatser/kommunikation/alternativ-och-kompletterande-kommunikation---akk/)). Withholding visual support to "force" speech tends to leave a child with no way to communicate at all.

## A support, not a whole plan

Bildstöd is low-tech, inexpensive, and well-evidenced. It is also, like [tydliggörande pedagogik](/blog/what-is-tydliggorande-pedagogik), a method that has to be prepared for each student and each situation, and that is where it quietly slips. When a teacher is stretched, the individualised picture schedule gets made for some students, some of the time, and the students who most need predictability get it least reliably. Visual support is often one concrete shape that a legally required [extra anpassning or särskilt stöd](/blog/what-do-extra-anpassningar-look-like) takes, which is also what makes it checkable: "this student has a visual schedule and a communication board" is a measure a school can put in place and follow up, not just a good intention. It belongs squarely inside [an accessible learning environment](/blog/what-is-an-accessible-learning-environment).

## Where Nuro fits

Nuro does not replace a communication board or a bildschema. Those belong to the people around the student, and they always will. What Nuro shares with bildstöd is the underlying principle: shrink the gap between what a lesson demands and what a student can process by adapting how information reaches them. Nuro does that for the learning content itself, per student, at a scale a stretched teacher cannot keep up by hand. The visual support in the room and the adapted material on the screen are two versions of the same idea, that a lesson built for thirty should still reach the one who takes information in differently.
