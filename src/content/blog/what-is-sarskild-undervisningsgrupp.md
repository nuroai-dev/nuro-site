## The short answer

A **särskild undervisningsgrupp** is a form of särskilt stöd where a student is taught "i en annan undervisningsgrupp än den som eleven normalt hör till", in a group other than the one they normally belong to. Its close relative, **enskild undervisning**, means the student is given "särskilt stöd enskilt", one to one. Both are legal, both require a formal decision, and both are deliberately framed by the law as measures of last resort.

For a neurodivergent student, a separate group can be the thing that finally makes school work, or it can be the moment inclusion quietly ends. Which one it is depends on whether the school reached for it as a considered step or as a fallback for a classroom it could not adapt. The law tries to keep it the former.

## What the law actually says

Both measures sit in **3 kap. 11 § skollagen**. The wording is narrow on purpose:

- A student may be placed in a särskild undervisningsgrupp or given enskild undervisning only "om det finns särskilda skäl", if there are special grounds.
- It is the **rektor** who decides, and this is one of the few decisions the rektor "får inte delegera", may not delegate. That is a signal of how serious the measure is.
- The decision is part of the student's [åtgärdsprogram](/blog/what-an-atgardsprogram-must-contain), the written plan that documents särskilt stöd. It does not happen informally.

The measure exists because for some students, and some needs, the ordinary group genuinely cannot deliver what they require, even with everything else in place. What the law will not accept is a school skipping the steps before it.

## It is meant to be the last option, not the first

Skolverket's guidance is explicit that this is among the school's most intrusive support measures, and that it must not be "mer ingripande än nödvändigt", more intrusive than necessary. A placement in a särskild undervisningsgrupp "ska ses som en lösning när alternativet att låta eleven gå kvar i den ordinarie undervisningsgruppen har prövats", should be seen as a solution once the alternative of keeping the student in the ordinary group has been tried.

That order matters. Before a separate group is on the table, the school is expected to have worked through the lighter tiers of support: [ledning och stimulans](/blog/what-is-ledning-och-stimulans) for everyone, then [extra anpassningar](/blog/what-do-extra-anpassningar-look-like) inside ordinary teaching, then särskilt stöd within the normal group. A särskild undervisningsgrupp is what remains when those have honestly been tried and are not enough, not a shortcut a school takes because adapting the ordinary classroom is hard.

This is why the measure is contested for neurodivergent students. Done well and by choice, a small, predictable, low-stimulus group can be exactly the environment an autistic or ADHD student needs to learn. Done as a default dumping ground for students a school lacks the capacity to include, it becomes segregation with a legal name. The same statute can produce both, so the surrounding process is everything.

## You can challenge the decision

Because it is a formal decision, it can be appealed. Skolverket is clear: "En rektors beslut om att en elev ska ges särskilt stöd i en särskild undervisningsgrupp får överklagas hos ÖKN", a rektor's decision to place a student in a särskild undervisningsgrupp may be appealed to Skolväsendets överklagandenämnd, under **28 kap. 16 § skollagen**. Students over 16 and guardians should be told this right when the decision is made. Decisions about enskild undervisning and [anpassad studiegång](/blog/what-is-anpassad-studiegang) can be appealed on the same basis. Our guide to [appealing a decision on särskilt stöd](/blog/how-to-appeal-a-sarskilt-stod-decision) walks through the deadline and the process.

If your objection is not to a specific decision but to a pattern, a school reaching for separation because it will not adapt, that is a different lever: [reporting the school to Skolinspektionen](/blog/how-to-report-a-school-to-skolinspektionen).

## Where Nuro fits

The honest aim of the tiered system is that far fewer students ever need a separate group, because the ordinary classroom was made to work for them. That is squarely Nuro's lane. When teaching material is adapted to how each student actually learns, lesson by lesson, more neurodivergent students can stay and succeed in the group they belong to, and a särskild undervisningsgrupp stays what the law intends: a genuine last resort for the few, not a fallback for a school without tools. And when a separate placement is genuinely the right call, the same continuous record of what a student needs and what has been tried is exactly what a lawful, well-documented decision, and any appeal, depends on.
