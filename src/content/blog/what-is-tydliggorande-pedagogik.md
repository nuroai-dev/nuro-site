## The short answer

Tydliggörande pedagogik, roughly "clarifying" or "explicit" teaching, is a way of organising the school day so that it is understandable and predictable rather than something a student has to decode on the fly. Instead of leaving expectations implicit, the teacher makes them visible: what we are doing, why, where, with whom, when, for how long, and what happens after. SPSM, Sweden's agency for special-needs education, describes it as building tydliga strukturer that make content tillgänglig och förutsägbar, accessible and predictable, using visual support and routine to lower the stress of not knowing ([SPSM](https://www.spsm.se/stod-och-rad/undervisning/autism-och-undervisning/)). Its roots are in TEACCH, an approach developed for autistic learners in the 1970s.

It sounds almost too simple. But for a neurodivergent student, uncertainty is not a minor irritation. It is the thing that burns the energy they need for learning.

## The questions it answers

The heart of the method is making sure a student can always answer a short set of basic questions about what is in front of them:

- **What am I supposed to do?**
- **Why am I doing it?**
- **Where does it happen?**
- **Who am I doing it with?**
- **When does it start, and how long will it last?**
- **How much is enough, and how do I know I am done?**
- **What happens after?**

A neurotypical student answers most of these automatically. A student who struggles with attention, or reads the room differently, or finds text slow to decode, often cannot, and the gap between "sort of understood the instruction" and "knew exactly what to do" is where the lesson quietly falls apart. A visual schedule, a written checklist, a clear start and end, a picture of the finished result: these are not decoration. They are the answers, made external.

## Why it helps beyond autism

Tydliggörande pedagogik grew out of autism practice, but the reason it works is executive function, and executive function is where [ADHD, autism and dyslexia overlap](/blog/when-a-child-has-more-than-one-diagnosis-npf-overlap). Planning, holding several steps in working memory, tracking time, starting a task: these are exactly the things a neurodivergent student tends to be short of. The method does not ask the student to be better at them. It moves the load off the student and onto the environment.

For a student with ADHD, a visible sequence and a clear finish line do the time-keeping and step-tracking their own working memory struggles to hold. For a student with dyslexia, clear structure and [visual support](/blog/what-is-bildstod-akk-visual-support) reduce how much unclear, text-heavy instruction they have to decode before they can even begin the actual task. Crucially, none of this lowers the bar. The student is working toward [the same goals as everyone else](/blog/autism-in-the-classroom-what-helps), with less friction in the way.

## From good practice to a legal duty

Here is the part schools miss. Tydliggörande pedagogik is not just pedagogy, it is often the concrete shape that legally required support takes. When [Skollagen's tiers of support](/blog/what-skollagen-requires-extra-anpassningar-sarskilt-stod) are put into practice, a visual schedule or a broken-down instruction is frequently exactly what [an extra anpassning looks like](/blog/what-do-extra-anpassningar-look-like), and for a student with an åtgärdsprogram, this kind of structure is often precisely [what the program prescribes and documents](/blog/what-an-atgardsprogram-must-contain).

That matters because it makes the support checkable. "Be clearer" is a wish. "This student has a written step-by-step for every independent task, a visual timer, and a defined end point" is a measure a school can put in place, follow up, and show it has provided.

## Why it does not happen by itself

The method is not hard to understand. It is hard to sustain, for every student who needs it, in every lesson. Preparing individualised clarity, the right visual support for this student, the instruction broken down the way this student needs, takes teacher time that most schools do not have to spare. So it happens for some students, some of the time, and the students who most need predictability get it least reliably.

That is the gap Nuro is built to close. When a teacher can generate the adapted, clarified version of a task for each student who needs it, and keep the record of what was provided, tydliggörande pedagogik stops being an ideal a few teachers manage on good days and becomes something every neurodivergent student can count on. The point is not less learning. It is the same learning, made possible.
