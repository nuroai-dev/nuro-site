## The short answer

Kravanpassning, demand adjustment, means matching what you ask of a student to what they can actually manage in that moment, so they can stay in the work and reach the goal, rather than being handed a task pitched above their current capacity and shutting down. SPSM, Sweden's specialist authority for education, frames it as getting the level right: when "kravnivån är rätt och innehållet är begripligt", the demand level is right and the content is comprehensible, it "skapar en känsla av sammanhang vilket främjar lärande och utveckling", it creates a sense of coherence that promotes learning and development ([SPSM](https://www.spsm.se/stod-och-rad/undervisning/adhd-och-undervisning/)).

The word gets misread constantly. "Anpassa kraven" is heard as "sänka kraven", lower the demands, as though the student is being let off. That is not what it means, and the difference is the whole point.

## Adapting the demand is not lowering the goal

Picture a student who has to write a text about the water cycle. The learning goal is that they understand and can explain the water cycle. The demand is everything the task asks of them on the way there: read a dense page, hold the structure in their head, plan paragraphs, spell, and produce it all in one sitting.

Lowering the goal would be deciding this student does not need to understand the water cycle. Kravanpassning leaves the goal untouched and adjusts the demand: break the task into steps, give the instruction in "korta och enkla instruktioner i få steg", short simple instructions in few steps, add visual support to "avlasta arbetsminnet", offload working memory, let them speak the answer instead of writing it, or split it across two shorter sessions. The destination is identical. The road is one the student can actually walk.

## Why neurodivergent students need it

For a student with ADHD, autism or dyslexia, the gap between a task and their capacity is often not about the goal at all. It is about executive load: getting started, holding several steps in working memory, keeping track of time, decoding text before the real thinking can even begin. A demand pitched above that capacity does not produce effort. It produces avoidance, overwhelm, or a stress reaction, and none of those are learning.

Right-sized demand does the opposite. It keeps the student inside a task they can succeed at, which is where learning and confidence both come from. This is also why kravanpassning is [the practical heart of lågaffektivt bemötande](/blog/what-is-lagaffektivt-bemotande): when a student is close to their limit, changing what is being asked is what brings the pressure down without a confrontation.

## What it looks like in practice

Most kravanpassning is small and concrete:

- **Break the task into steps** and reveal them one at a time, so the whole thing does not land at once. This is the same move as [tydliggörande pedagogik](/blog/what-is-tydliggorande-pedagogik).
- **Change the format**, not the content: speak instead of write, listen instead of read, point to a symbol instead of speak. This is where [bildstöd and AKK](/blog/what-is-bildstod-akk-visual-support) do their work.
- **Adjust the amount at once**: five problems now, not twenty; one paragraph, then a break.
- **Adjust the timing**, matching harder demands to the times of day a student has more in the tank.
- **Give a clear start and end**, so "how much and for how long" is never a mystery.

## The line that matters

There is a real risk on the other side. Kravanpassning done badly slides into permanently expecting less of a student, which is its own way of failing them, and it is why the method has critics. The test is simple: are you adjusting the demand to reach the same goal, or quietly abandoning the goal? Good demand adjustment is temporary scaffolding aimed at the same destination as everyone else, and it is often exactly the concrete content of a legally required [extra anpassning or särskilt stöd](/blog/what-do-extra-anpassningar-look-like). The student is working toward [the same knowledge goals as the rest of the class](/blog/autism-in-the-classroom-what-helps), with less friction in the way.

## Where Nuro fits

Right-sizing the demand for one student, on one task, is not hard to understand. Doing it for every student who needs it, in every subject, in every lesson, is where it breaks down, because it takes teacher time that no school has to spare. Nuro is built for exactly that. It takes the assignment the class is already doing and adapts the demand per student, breaking it into steps, changing the format, pacing it, while keeping the goal fixed, so the lesson designed for thirty still reaches the one who needs the road broken down. The goal never moves. Only the friction does.
