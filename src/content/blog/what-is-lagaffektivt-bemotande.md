## The short answer

Lågaffektivt bemötande, usually translated as the low-arousal approach, is a way of responding to challenging or overwhelmed behaviour that was developed and popularised in Sweden by psychologist Bo Hejlskov Elvén. It rests on a single sentence: "Människor som kan uppföra sig gör det", people who are able to behave, do. When a student does not, the approach assumes they lacked the conditions to meet the demand in that moment, not the will ([Bo Hejlskov Elvén](https://hejlskov.se/lagaffektivt-bemotande/)). So the adult's task is to bring the pressure down and help the student keep control, rather than escalate a confrontation that the student is already losing.

It is not about being permissive. It is about being effective in the moment a student is close to tipping over.

## Behaviour as a sign of exceeded capacity

The core reframe is that difficult behaviour is usually coping under stress, not defiance. A student with ADHD who cannot sit still, an autistic student reacting to sensory overload or an unexpected change, a student with dyslexia who becomes angry rather than start a task they cannot read: from the front of the room these look like discipline problems. More often they are a sign that the situation asked more than the student could give right then. Punishing the reaction does nothing about the cause, and usually adds stress, which makes the next reaction more likely.

## The three principles

Lågaffektivt bemötande is built on three principles that all point the adult, not the student, toward the change:

- **Ansvarsprincipen, responsibility.** "Den som tar ansvar kan påverka", the one who takes responsibility can influence the situation. You cannot order a dysregulated student to calm down, but you can adjust the demand and remove stressors. This adjustment, kravanpassning, is the practical heart of the method: change what is being asked so the student can succeed.
- **Kontrollprincipen, control.** You have to keep control of yourself before you can help someone else regain theirs. Raising your voice or squaring up hands your own stress to a student who has none to spare.
- **Affektsmitta, affect contagion.** Emotion spreads. A calm voice, relaxed posture, giving space, and not demanding eye contact spread calm; a stressed adult spreads stress. In practice the method leans on diversion, affect regulation, and demand adjustment rather than confrontation.

## Why it matters for keeping students in school

A student who is sent out, held, or shamed for a stress reaction learns that school is a place where the hardest moments are met with more pressure. Repeated often enough, that lesson is one of the quiet roads toward avoidance, and eventually toward becoming a [hemmasittare, a student who has stopped coming at all](/blog/school-absence-starts-earlier). De-escalation does the opposite. It keeps the student in the room, keeps the relationship intact, and keeps the incident from becoming the thing the student remembers about school. The [cost of losing a student to absence](/blog/cost-of-school-absence-false-economy) is far higher than the cost of adjusting a demand.

## A response, not a substitute for support

This is where lågaffektivt bemötande is easy to misread. It is how you respond in the moment, not a reason to lower expectations forever or to stop asking why the moments keep happening. It works best paired with prevention. Predictable structure, the kind [tydliggörande pedagogik](/blog/what-is-tydliggorande-pedagogik) provides, means fewer situations tip a student past their capacity in the first place, and tasks pitched at the right level mean the demand rarely outruns what the student can do. And when disruption keeps repeating, [Skollagen treats it as a signal to investigate the need behind it](/blog/trygghet-och-studiero-when-behaviour-is-a-signal), not simply a behaviour to manage. Calm responses and real support are not alternatives. The first buys the time to provide the second.

## Where Nuro fits

Nuro works on the prevention side of that pairing. When each student's tasks are adapted to the right level and the signs of struggle surface early, there are fewer moments where a student is pushed past capacity and into a reaction, and there is a record that lets a school treat those moments as a support question rather than a discipline file. The calm response still belongs to the adult in the room. Nuro's job is to make the room ask less of a student than they can bear less often.
